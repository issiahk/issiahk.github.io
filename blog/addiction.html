Addiction

By Leo Webb
Published on June 26th, 2020

How would you combat a game, drug or internet addiction if you suspect you or someone you know is down the hole?

Conventional wisdom, including the current stance of many police forces across the world, is that those who are addicted to drugs or alcohol have made the choice to be that way, to be seen as a stain on society, when it is generally not the case; imagine yourself in a situation on the precipice of an addiction - you wouldn’t want to choose to be ostracised, to waste away your future, especially given that in this situation you are already aware of the consequences of becoming dependent on something. Many people don’t have this moment of choice. The key change in mentality to make is to recognise that addiction is a mental illness, a compulsion, something that the victims of which often want to escape and simply need outside support to do so.

An addict’s mind is not as single-track as one might assume. The person before the addiction is not completely in control; it feels like a car without brakes: the driver might clearly see the cliff in the distance, but it’s not like they can get out of that situation on their own. After some length of time indulging themselves, a large number of addicts will think, in the back of their head, thoughts like:

I don’t want to play this next game…

I shouldn’t have had that drink…

I should have stopped watching this an hour ago…

...and it will still be nonetheless excruciating to pull away, and they will keep on consuming whatever it is that keeps them their servant; it needs to be understood that these doubting thoughts, in agreement with the advice and opinions of the people around them, are needed for recovery to initiate.

The notion that “change has to come from the inside” can be demoralising if you are concerned about someone close to you who is slipping into the throes of an obsession. However, this does not mean that you are powerless - one approach often used by social workers and concerned relations alike is very similar to a method used of deprogramming members of a cult; if you feel the addict in question will respond well, you could say positive, reassuring statements to the person in question, disregarding their authenticity, for example: “I’m glad you feel that smoking is increasing your self-esteem and making you feel more confident.” Some people of inquisitive nature may find themselves thinking about what you say to them, questioning the statements’ truth, and planting the seeds of initiative.

On the other hand, you know the people you care about and you can sometimes tell whether such a technique is likely to work on them; in these cases it’s often better to provide alternative uses of their time, energy and money - “distractions”, “hobbies”, “options”, whatever you would call them, they are a reliable way to nudge someone out of an obsession. This is because, when in the process of escaping an addiction, people who have more time and energy to spare are many times more vulnerable to relapsing; the phrase “idle hands do the devil’s work”, although demonizing of the afflicted, rings especially true here, and I can confirm this from my own experience with a gaming addiction. Good choices for alternative activities would both:

Be easy to grow into more time-consuming hobbies, for example playing an instrument, where there is always room to practice and improve as much as you want,

As well as be something the people around them can share or take part in, such as a team sport, which makes it less likely that they will relapse.

It can be tempting to accompany this with a cold-turkey removal of the addictive thing from someone’s life, especially when they are being hurt by it. However, it is important to know (especially with regards to alcohol) that the withdrawal symptoms for alcohol (tremors, nausea, headaches and so on experienced by someone who suddenly stops habitual drinking) can be quite serious and you should consult with a medical professional about whether someone who wants to quit the drug should do so under hospital supervision. This caution should apply equally to other drugs as well; be informed before making the decision.

The issue of addiction is important, given its prevalence among the teenagers of HK today, especially in international schools, and how much time, energy, and potential for the future it takes away from our youth. I hope that, with enough hard work, enough people can learn about it to make it a thing of the past.
