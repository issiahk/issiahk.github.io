Why Refugees Should Be Given the Right to Vaccinate in Hong Kong

By John Zhang
Published on May 23rd, 2021

"We already face enough discrimination and stereotypes from the public, why exclude us further when all we ask for is that our personal safety and health be cared for just as equally as a Hong Kong Resident?” - 19 year old asylum seeker

This article revolves around the following petition: http://chng.it/KPFHvCRV. If you agree with these views, please be sure to sign and share to advocate for this cause!

Claiming asylum in Hong Kong is a process bound with stringent laws and regulations. Claimants are first required to overstay their visa they entered Hong Kong with, and then file to officially claim non-refoulement through the Unified Screening System. However, the time needed for the screening process to conduct can often take up to several years.

In the meantime, asylum seekers in Hong Kong are essentially powerless: because this status doesn’t classify them as an official citizen, they are devoid of rights such as the ability to work or receive the COVID vaccination. While government subsidies - 1,500 HKD for housing and 1,200 for food- are provided, the average price for a subdivided flat according to the NGO Branches of Hope is 5000 HKD, and the insufficiency of their food allowance often forces them to rely on NGOs for these necessities.

Among other hazards, the biggest risk refugees face living in these subdivided houses - on average 5.3 square meters for a small suite according to the Legislative Council - are the cramped living areas and shared spaces in our current COVID climate. Despite social distancing being of utmost importance, refugees are forced into close vicinity with other residents. Escaping a potential infection becomes near impossible without the protection of a vaccine, and is even more likely to cause an outbreak.

Providing the vaccination to refugees would further benefit society as a whole. Despite the vaccine being available to most HK residents, the take-up rate is still extremely low with only 17 percent of Hongkongers having received the first dose. This is in contrast with the recommended number of at least 70% of the population being vaccinated in order to achieve herd immunity, according to the UNHCR.

There is no lack in supply to also accommodate for the refugee population of around 13,000. They should be especially provided for due to their high risk situation, and given the right to a vaccination like any other Hong Kong resident. With a greater proportion of the population vaccinated, this will benefit all HK residents in the long term.
